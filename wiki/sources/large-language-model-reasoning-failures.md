# Large Language Model Reasoning Failures

## Summary

This paper presents the first comprehensive survey of reasoning failures in Large Language Models (LLMs), introducing a novel taxonomy that distinguishes reasoning types and failure categories. It analyzes fundamental, application-specific, and robustness-related reasoning failures across embodied and non-embodied reasoning, and explores their root causes and mitigation strategies. The survey unifies fragmented research, highlights common patterns, and provides a structured perspective to guide future improvements in LLM reasoning reliability.

## Key Points

- Introduces a two-axis taxonomy: reasoning type (embodied vs. non-embodied, informal vs. formal) and failure type (fundamental, application-specific, robustness).
- Analyzes core reasoning failures in LLMs, including deficits in cognitive skills, cognitive biases, Theory of Mind, social norms, and multi-agent planning.
- Discusses root causes (architecture, training, data, alignment) and mitigation strategies (prompting, data curation, adversarial training, system-level interventions).

## Concepts Extracted

- **[[Taxonomy Of LLM Reasoning Failures]]** — A structured framework for categorizing reasoning failures in Large Language Models (LLMs), distinguishing between embodied and non-embodied reasoning, and classifying failures as fundamental, application-specific, or robustness-related. This taxonomy enables systematic analysis and comparison of reasoning weaknesses, guiding research and mitigation strategies.
- **[[Cognitive Biases In Large Language Models]]** — Cognitive biases are systematic deviations from rational judgment observed in both humans and Large Language Models (LLMs). These biases arise from mental shortcuts, limited cognitive resources, or contextual influences, and can significantly affect LLM reasoning performance across diverse tasks.
- **[[Theory Of Mind In Large Language Models]]** — Theory of Mind (ToM) is the cognitive ability to attribute mental states to oneself and others, enabling interpretation of behaviors and prediction of actions. In LLMs, ToM reasoning is evaluated through tasks that require understanding beliefs, intentions, and emotions, but current models exhibit significant limitations and inconsistencies.
